Session 1 · 9:30 AM
WHY
MUSIC
MATTERS.
The principle
MUSIC IS A
CREATIVE
DECISION.
The instrument you choose tells the audience what to feel before a single visual registers. The brain processes music faster than image. Your score arrives first.
This means your music is not background. It is the emotional argument your film makes. Every instrument choice is a statement about your client's brand.
A solo piano says something different than a full orchestra. A banjo says something different than a string quartet. Neither is better — both are specific. Specificity is the craft.

Three questions before you score

  • What does this scene need the audience to feel?
  • What does my client's brand sound like in the world?
  • Is this music choice serving the scene or my personal taste?

The most misunderstood point
SOCA IS NOT
JUST CARNIVAL.
The rhythm pattern of soca — the kick on one and three, the specific hi-hat pattern — creates energy and forward momentum that works across contexts when stripped of the full production.
The three soca registers
Full soca — brass stabs, call and response — carnival context only
Soca rhythm + orchestral — Caribbean corporate. Proud and professional.
Soca pattern + ambient pads — modern, sophisticated, identifiably ours

Also consider

  • Calypso guitar (syncopated) — social commentary instrument. Makes people think about things they resist. IRD's civic messaging.
  • Reggae one-drop (kick on 3) — space is the point. Reliable, inevitable, unhurried. Permanence without urgency.
  • Chutney influences — Caribbean music identity is plural. Acknowledging that is itself a creative statement.

The discipline
VO LENGTH
DICTATES
MUSIC.
Your voiceover length determines everything about how the music is structured. Music and voice are not independent — they breathe together. When the voice speaks, music steps back. When the voice stops, music reclaims the space.
This is not a technical rule. It is a conversation between two storytelling instruments. Your job is to conduct that conversation.

The rule of relationship

  • When VO speaks — music drops 20–30% volume
  • When VO pauses — music breathes back up
  • When VO ends — music rises to close
  • VO under 10s — music carries full melody before and after
  • VO 10–20s — music must be textural. Melody competes with voice.
  • VO over 20s — music almost disappears. Rhythm and atmosphere only.

The formula
THE SUNO
PROMPT
FORMULA.
Five components — always include all five
MOOD The emotional register — cinematic, tense, warm, joyful, mournful, urgent, whimsical
INSTRUMENT Specific instruments — not "strings" but "solo cello". Not "Caribbean" but "steel pan as harmonic texture under jazz piano trio"
ENERGY Tempo and movement — slow building, steady pulse, fast cut, gentle forward momentum, driving urgency

Your melody. Suno's production.
HUM IT.
UPLOAD IT.
BUILD IT.

What this does

You don't have to describe your music in words. If you have a melody in your head — hum it, record it, upload it. Suno builds a full production around your melodic idea while preserving your contour, rhythm, and feel.

This is the bridge between your musical intuition and AI production. You bring the idea. Suno brings the band.

What works as a seed

  • A hummed melody — even just "bum bum bum bum"
  • A rhythm tapped on a desk and recorded
  • You singing the hook a cappella
  • A chord on a guitar, piano, or ukulele
  • Any short audio clip — 5 to 10 seconds is ideal

What to avoid

  • Clips longer than 30 seconds — Suno drifts from the seed
  • Very noisy recordings — background noise confuses the model
  • Uploading someone else's music — Suno's terms prohibit this
  • Leaving the style prompt blank — always tell Suno what to build

Step by step

  • Step 1 — Open your phone voice memo app
  • Step 2 — Hum your melody. 5-10 seconds. Short and clear.
  • Step 3 — Open Suno. Click Create. Find the audio upload icon.
  • Step 4 — Upload your voice memo
  • Step 5 — Add a style prompt — tell Suno what to build around your melody
  • Step 6 — Generate. Two variations. Listen to both.

When Suno Sings
LYRICS &
JINGLES.

Instrumental vs Lyrical — know the difference

  • Instrumental score — music under visuals. Supports the image. Never competes. No words.
  • Lyrical underscoring — a full song playing under visuals. Lyrics comment thematically — not about the brand directly.
  • Jingle — short, melodic, branded. Has the client's name or tagline baked in. Designed to repeat and stick.
  • Spoken word / rap — powerful for youth-facing content. Claude writes the bars, Suno delivers them.

Which clients suit jingles

  • ECCB — financial literacy jingle for young people. Learn your credit score through a hook.
  • IRD Grenada / IRD Anguilla — calypso jingle. Sparrow and Kitchener made social commentary through song for exactly this reason.
  • Genzcarwash — Gen Z brand jingle. Soca or hip-hop influenced. Plays in the tunnel. Sticks in your head.
  • SCASPA — a maritime anthem with a chorus. Gateway to the world energy.
  • Outsource Dev — a short brand theme. "Making business better" — memorable and human.

The workflow — Claude writes, Suno sings

  • Claude writes the lyrics first — always
  • Brief Claude with: client name, key message, hook keywords, audience, style, duration
  • Claude returns full lyrics with verse, chorus, and bridge if needed
  • Paste lyrics into Suno's custom lyrics field — not the style prompt
  • Add style descriptors separately: genre, tempo, voice type, instrumentation
  • Generate. Iterate. The hook should be singable after one listen.
