What to listen for

  • The loudest sound in the room
  • The quietest sound you can detect
  • Any sounds that repeat rhythmically
  • The quality of the silence between sounds
  • Anything that tells you exactly where you are

  • Every space has a sonic identity
  • Silence is never really silence

03

Everything else. The physical world the music and voice live inside. Ambient sound, room tone, foley, SFX. What makes the world feel real even when every image is AI-generated.
Ambient 10–15% · SFX 60–80%
A beautifully scored film with no ambient sound feels like a video game cutscene. Sound design is what makes the world feel inhabited.
Sound design concept — 01
ROOM
TONE.
Room tone is the silence that isn't silence. Every space has a sound — a constant low-level audio fingerprint that tells you exactly where you are without showing you anything.
A port has a specific room tone. A boardroom has a different one. A car wash tunnel has its own. A government office at 8am has another. Without room tone, your scenes feel like they exist in a vacuum.
Room tone sits under everything — under the score, under the VO, under the SFX. At 10–15% volume it is never consciously noticed. But take it away and the scene immediately feels wrong.
Port at work
Engine hum, distant crane, rope and water, occasional maritime radio. Low and constant.
SCASPA — all port scenes
Government office
Air conditioning hum, keyboard clicks, distant phone, muffled corridor voices.
IRD Grenada · IRD Anguilla
Caribbean outdoors
Wind through palms, distant ocean, birds, occasional vehicle passing. Never completely quiet.
Any establishing exterior shot
Car wash tunnel
Mechanical wash sounds, water pressure, conveyor hum, bass frequencies reverberating.

Sound design concept — 02
FOLEY.
Foley is sound effects that match physical actions on screen. Named after Jack Foley — the pioneer who realised that recorded dialogue always needs physical sound added back in post-production.
When a character in your film signs a document, the camera might show their hand. But the audience needs to hear the pen on paper, the slight rustle of the document. Without it, the moment feels oddly distant.
For AI-generated brand films, foley is particularly important — because AI visuals have no natural sound. Every sound in your film is a deliberate choice.
Paper signing
Pen on paper, slight document rustle. ECCB account opening, IRD filing.
Keyboard clicks
Professional and deliberate. CUB digital banking, Outsource Dev tech scenes.
Footsteps on tarmac
Confident stride. SCASPA airport and port arrival scenes.
Car door close
Solid and satisfying. Genzcarwash arrival. The premium sound of a well-built car.
Handshake
Warm, firm. Outsource Dev business relationship scenes.
Phone notification
Digital, modern. CUB mobile banking alert. IRD tax deadline reminder.
Sound design concept — 03
AMBIENT
BED.
The ambient bed is the constant background sound layer. It is not music. It is not SFX. It is atmosphere — the sonic environment your film lives inside.
Unlike room tone which is tied to a specific location, the ambient bed can evolve across the film. An outdoor market transitions to an office interior — the ambient bed changes with the scene but continues without interruption.
Run at 10–15% volume. If your audience notices it consciously, it's too loud. The ambient bed should only be noticed when it's removed.
Ocean — Caribbean
Not Hawaiian ocean. Not Mediterranean ocean. Caribbean ocean has a specific wave pattern, a different wind quality. If you're recording this yourself — record it here.

Sound design concept — 04
SFX —
PUNCTUATION.
SFX are specific sounds that punctuate specific moments. Unlike the ambient bed which runs constantly, SFX drop in at precise timestamps to mark an action, transition, or emphasis point.
Think of SFX as the sonic equivalent of a cut. A camera cut creates visual punctuation — an SFX creates sonic punctuation at the same moment. Together they compound the impact.
Run at 60–80% volume — present and deliberate, but never so loud they pull focus from the VO or score.
Whoosh
Scene transition. Logo reveal. Camera sweep. The most used SFX in brand film.
Cash register
Financial transaction. IRD payment received. CUB transfer confirmed.
Aircraft engine
Jet approaching or departing. SCASPA runway scenes. Arrival and departure.
Notification ping
Digital confirmation. Task complete. Modern and satisfying. CUB digital banking.
Door open
Entry. Beginning. Possibility. Any scene where someone steps into a new space.
Water spray
Car wash pressure wash. The satisfying hit of water on a vehicle. Genzcarwash core SFX.

Source 01 — The Library
FREESOUND

The largest free SFX library on the internet. Community-contributed, Creative Commons licensed. Over 600,000 sounds — rain, ocean, ports, offices, vehicles, foley, everything.
Searchable. Download as MP3 or WAV. Import directly into CapCut.

Source 03 — The Real Thing
RECORD IT
YOURSELF.
The most authentic option. A phone recording of the actual ambient sound of St. Kitts, Dominica, Grenada, Antigua, Anguilla, or Saint Lucia. That specificity is cultural authenticity embedded in the audio.
Caribbean ocean waves in St. Kitts sound different from ocean waves in Dominica. The market in Roseau sounds different from the market in Basseterre. A phone recording captures that difference. No library can.
The remote advantage: your remote students ARE on their islands right now. A student in Grenada can open their window and record the ambient sound of Grenada. That sound cannot be found on Freesound.org.

How to record well on a phone

  • Find a quiet spot away from wind hitting the mic directly
  • Hold the phone still — movement creates handling noise
  • Record at least 60 seconds — you'll trim it down
  • Record during the time of day that matches your scene
  • Don't hold your breath — natural breathing is fine
  • Label it immediately: "Grenada_port_morning_07-21.m4a"

Session 3 · 11:30 AM
CARIBBEAN
SOUND
IDENTITY.
Beyond music
WHAT THE
CARIBBEAN
SOUNDS LIKE.

Natural sounds

  • Rain on a zinc roof — one of the most distinctively Caribbean sounds that exists. Instantly places your viewer.
  • Caribbean ocean — not Hawaiian, not Mediterranean. Our wave pattern and wind quality is specific.
  • Wind through palms — a different sound from wind through pine or oak. Our palms. Our wind.
  • Tropical birds — bananaquit, hummingbird, morning doves. Our birds sound like our islands.
  • Afternoon heat — the ambient buzz of a hot Caribbean afternoon. Cicadas and stillness.

Human sounds

  • Market vendor calls — the specific cadence of Caribbean vendors in Creole, patois, or English
  • Steel band warming up — distant, not performance. The casual practice sound.
  • Church bells on Sunday — every Caribbean island has this. Civic and communal.
  • Maxi taxi / minibus — the sound of how we move. Horn, engine, passengers.
  • Port radio chatter — VHF marine radio. The sound of working boats.

Why this matters for AI film

Your visuals are AI-generated. They look real but they were never anywhere. They have no inherent location. They could be set anywhere in the world.
The sounds of your island — placed underneath those visuals — are what claim the film for the Caribbean. A Caribbean viewer hearing rain on a zinc roof under an AI-generated office scene does not question whether that office is in their world.
Sound is how you place your film in the Caribbean without showing the Caribbean.
🔴
Red Alert
Don't use generic tropical sounds from a library and call it Caribbean. Maui ocean is not our ocean. Bali rain is not our rain. Your island has a specific sound. Find it, record it, use it.

The test
THE
AUTHENTICITY
TEST.
Before you lock any ambient sound — play it to someone from that island. Ask them one question:
"Does this sound like home?"

If the answer is yes

  • Lock it in. That sound is yours.
  • Note what specifically made it feel right
  • Use that knowledge for the rest of your scenes

If the answer is no

  • Find out what's wrong — too generic? Wrong birds? Wrong ocean?
  • Go back to ElevenLabs and be more specific in your prompt
  • Or ask your remote teammate to record the real thing tonight

THE AUDIO
STACK.
The production framework
FOUR LAYERS
IN
CAPCUT.
Layer 1 — SCORE
Suno track · Bottom layer · Full length of film
Layer 2 — VOICEOVER
ElevenLabs MP3 · Always 100% · Nothing competes
Layer 3 — AMBIENT BED
Room tone / island ambient · 10–15% always · Never noticed
Layer 4 — SFX
Punctuation moments · 60–80% · Specific timestamps only
These four layers repeat for every scene in your film. Import them all into CapCut. Arrange on the timeline. The bars above show their relative volume relationships — not a fixed rule, but a strong starting point.
The mix
VOLUME
RELATIONSHIPS.
Volume in a film mix is not absolute — it's relational. Every layer's volume is defined by its relationship to every other layer. The VO is always the anchor. Everything else is measured against it.
Score — when VO speaks
Drop to 25–30%. The music should feel like it stepped aside to let the narrator speak. Not gone — present but respectful.
Score — when VO stops
Rise back to 80–100%. The music reclaims the space. This moment of the music "breathing back" is one of the most satisfying moments in a well-mixed film.
Ambient Bed — always
10–15% throughout. Never changes. If you notice it, it's too loud. Test: mute it for 3 seconds, then unmute. You should feel the difference — not hear a specific change.
SFX — at moment of action
60–80% at the exact timestamp. Drop to zero immediately after. SFX should feel like punctuation — present and then gone.

The CapCut workflow

  • Import all four audio files as separate tracks
  • Layer them: Score → Ambient → SFX → VO (VO always on top)
  • Set the base volumes first — don't automate yet
  • Play back the full 30 seconds
  • Add volume keyframes where the VO begins and ends — duck the score there
  • Add SFX at their precise timestamps
  • Play back again. Close your eyes and just listen.
  • Ask: does this sound like the world your client operates in?
